# Pre-Exhibition Critique: How It Works

**FVEA-488-01: VibeCoding with AI | Program in Experimental Animation | CalArts**
**Instructor: Doug Goodwin**

This document is for everyone in the room. Every role — creator, respondent, moderator — is described here so that everyone knows what is expected of everyone else. Fairness depends on transparency.

---

## What This Is For

This is a working critique — the last structured feedback session before the Bijou Festival. The goal is to strengthen each student's piece for public exhibition. Every response in the room should leave the student with something concrete to act on.

At Bijou, 10 students will share 30 minutes of screen time with an audience that includes the executive director of CCAT: The CalArts Chanel Initiative. There will be no crit at the festival. The work must arrive ready.

---

## Session Format

**Duration:** ~90 minutes
**Presenters:** 10 students
**Structure:** ~8 minutes per student, plus a closing round

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0:00 – 0:30 | Creator reads their statement |
| 0:30 – 1:30 | First screening |
| 1:30 – 2:30 | Second screening — same piece, no talking |
| 2:30 – 3:00 | Silence — 30 seconds, no talking |
| 3:00 – 3:30 | Creator reads their two questions |
| 3:30 – 8:30 | Directed feedback |

**Why two screenings?** The first viewing is acclimation — you're adjusting to what the piece is. The second is when you actually see it. Comments based on a single pass of time-based work tend to be general impressions, not specific observations. Don't speak until both screenings are done.

**Why the silence?** After the second screening, the room sits for 30 seconds without speaking. This is not awkward — it is the room processing what it just saw. Michael Asher built his legendary CalArts Post-Studio crit around the principle that silence does work. You don't have his fifteen hours. But you can give the piece thirty seconds to settle before the conversation begins.

## For Creators

### Before the Crit

You will prepare five things (see the separate prep assignment for full details):

1. **MP4 recording** of your piece (30–60 seconds)
2. **Opening statement** (100 words max: title, source material, synthesis stratum, key techniques)
3. **Prompt log excerpt** (2–3 pivotal AI exchanges)
4. **Two questions for the room** (one about the work, one about the system)
5. **One thing you'd change** (one sentence, on the back of your question card)

### During the Crit

**Read your statement, then step back.** You set the frame; the room does the rest. Don't explain or apologize while the piece screens.

**Listen more than you talk.** When people give you feedback, your job is to take it in, not to defend. If you disagree, note it silently — you may find they were right when you watch the piece again later. If a comment confuses you, ask them to say more. If it stings, sit with it.

**Name the labor.** Vibecoded work can look effortless. A visually complex shader can emerge from what appears to be a short conversation. If you spent twelve hours steering, resetting, and iterating, say so. If you don't, the room may unconsciously give your work less attention than it deserves — Elkins calls this the effort-calibration problem. Your prompt log is evidence. Use it.

**Use your rescue card.** If feedback stalls or goes in circles, pull out the "one thing you'd change" and redirect: "Here's what I'm actually stuck on."

### After the Crit

You will leave with a clear takeaway — the moderator will synthesize it. Write it down. You have limited time before Bijou. Act on the one or two things that will make the biggest difference.

## For Respondents

Everyone in the room is a respondent when they are not presenting. This is the hardest role, and it matters the most.

### How to Watch

Watch the piece twice before forming your response. During the first screening, just let it happen. During the second, look for specifics: a moment, a transition, a color, a rhythm, a feeling. Anchor your response in something you can point to.

### How to Respond

**Answer the creator's questions first.** They wrote those questions because they need those specific answers. Start there before offering your own observations.

**Name before you interpret.** Describe the visual event, then say what it does to you. "The particles contract toward center at 0:15 and the color shifts from blue to warm orange" is observation. "It feels like breathing" is interpretation. Do the first one first.

**Be specific.** "I like it" is not feedback. "The moment at 0:40 where the symmetry breaks feels intentional — that asymmetry is the most alive the piece gets" is feedback.

**Aim at Bijou.** Every comment should help the creator improve the piece for exhibition. "This is interesting" helps no one. "If you extended that transition by two seconds, it would land harder in a screening context" helps.

**Don't retreat to technique.** It is always safe to talk about noise functions, UV coordinates, and parameter tuning. No one's sense of themselves ever got bruised by a conversation about smoothstep. But safe feedback is not useful feedback. Push yourself to say what the piece *does to you* — where it holds your attention, where it loses you, what it reminds you of, what feeling it produces. That is what the Bijou audience will experience, and that is what the creator needs to hear.

**One lens is enough.** A focused response through a single lens (see below) beats a scattered tour of all three.

---

## Three Critical Lenses

These give everyone a shared vocabulary. You do not need to use all three in a single response.

### Lens 1: Formal Observation — What is in the work?

Respond to the piece as a composition. Color, motion, rhythm, density, pacing, emotional register. Where does your eye go? Where does your attention hold, and where does it drop? What moment is strongest?

The tool doesn't matter here. The image does.

### Lens 2: System Analysis — How is it organized?

What appears authored by the student, and what appears generated? Where is the student's hand visible — in parameter choices, framing, pacing, source material? Are the seams between direction and generation legible? Is that legibility intentional?

This lens asks about control. Vibecoding is steering, not typing. The question is whether the student is driving.

### Lens 3: Consequences — What is entangled?

What source material and labor are embedded in the output? Does the piece acknowledge or obscure its origins? This lens is not about guilt — it is about awareness, and it may surface choices that strengthen the work's integrity for a public audience.

## For the Moderator

The moderator keeps the session useful. The enemy is drift — toward praise, toward technique, toward abstraction, away from the work.

**Keep time visibly.** A gentle signal at 7:00 gives respondents a chance to land. Hard stop at 8:30.

**Hold the silence.** The 30 seconds after the second screening will feel long. Let it be long. Do not fill it. The room is doing work — processing the piece, forming responses that are more than first reactions. Asher's Post-Studio crit ran on the principle that when there is nothing to say, that itself becomes the question. You don't need fifteen hours to use silence. You need thirty seconds of not rushing past it.

**Synthesize after each creator.** "So the two things to work on before Bijou are _____ and _____." This gives the student a takeaway they can act on.

**Watch for the technique trap.** In critiques of technically interesting work, the room can spend the entire session talking about process — noise fields, prompt strategies, parameter spaces — while avoiding what the piece actually means or how it feels. If the conversation has been in Lens 2 for more than two minutes without touching Lens 1, redirect: "We've been talking about how this was made. What does it feel like to watch?"

**Name the labor when the room doesn't see it.** If the prompt log excerpt reveals significant iteration, false summits, or hard creative decisions, surface it. The room needs to know the work is there in order to engage with it seriously.

**Welcome unexpected responses.** Peers may react to a shader the way they'd react to a film, a painting, or a piece of music. Those interdisciplinary responses are closer to what the Bijou audience will experience. Don't correct them toward the "right" vocabulary — let them stand.

**Use the last ten minutes.** After all 10 creators, open the floor for observations about the body of work as a whole: patterns, range, shared strengths, shared gaps. What does this collection of pieces say about vibecoding as a practice? That question is worth posing before showing the work publicly.

## Context: What Is VibeCoding?

For visitors or reviewers unfamiliar with the course:

**VibeCoding with AI** is a course in CalArts' Program in Experimental Animation. Students write real-time graphics programs (GLSL shaders) by conversing with AI language models — describing what they want to see, reading the generated code, testing it visually, and iterating. The student provides creative direction and critical judgment; the AI provides syntax and technical scaffolding.

This is not image generation — students are not typing a prompt and receiving a finished picture. They are writing and modifying code through sustained dialogue, making compositional decisions at every step: what to keep, what to discard, when to reset, how to steer. Their prompt logs — records of these conversations — are part of the coursework and reveal the depth of creative labor involved.

Work is made in **shader-playground**, a browser-based platform with interactive controls and built-in video recording. Students have progressed from basic color gradients through noise functions, particle systems, raymarched 3D forms, and fluid simulations.

The course situates this practice within labor history, intellectual property discourse, and the lineage of experimental animation at CalArts. Students are expected not only to make work, but to articulate where it comes from and what is at stake in how they use these tools.
