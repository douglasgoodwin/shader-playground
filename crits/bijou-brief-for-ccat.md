# VibeCoding with AI: Bijou Festival Brief

**For MaryCLare, Executive Director, CCAT: The CalArts Chanel Initiative**
**From Doug Goodwin, Program in Experimental Animation**
**Spring 2026**

---

## What You'll See at Bijou

Ten students from FVEA-488-01, *VibeCoding with AI*, will screen 30 minutes of original real-time animation. Each student has approximately 3 minutes to show a short piece — a recorded shader, running between 30 and 60 seconds — that they created through conversational programming with AI.

The pieces range across particle systems, fluid simulations, raymarched 3D forms, procedural landscapes, and abstract visual music. They are not AI-generated images. They are programs — authored, iterated, and directed by the students through sustained dialogue with language models.

---

## What VibeCoding Is

VibeCoding is a creative practice in which students write real-time graphics programs (GLSL shaders) by conversing with AI language models. The student describes what they want to see. The AI writes code. The student tests it, sees the result, and steers the next iteration. This loop — describe, generate, evaluate, redirect — continues until the piece emerges.

The student provides creative direction, compositional judgment, and critical awareness. The AI provides syntax and technical scaffolding. Neither can make the work alone.

This is meaningfully different from AI image generation, where a user types a prompt and receives a finished picture. In vibecoding, students read and modify code, make decisions at every step, encounter failure regularly, and develop a practice of iterative creative problem-solving. Their prompt logs — transcripts of the conversations that produced each piece — are part of the coursework and reveal the depth of labor involved.

---

## What the Students Have Learned

The course spans 16 weeks, progressing from basic color and coordinate systems through:

- Mathematical drawing (shapes, distance fields, symmetry)
- Animation and time-based composition
- Procedural noise and organic texture
- 3D rendering through raymarching
- Particle systems and emergent behavior
- Fluid dynamics and reaction-diffusion
- Audio-reactive visualization

Students work in **shader-playground**, a browser-based platform built for the course with interactive parameter controls and built-in video recording. Each week introduces new techniques through scaffolded exercises, which students then extend through their own vibecoding sessions.

## How Students Are Preparing

Before Bijou, students go through a structured pre-exhibition critique in class. Each student presents their work-in-progress, and the room provides directed feedback aimed at strengthening the piece for public showing.

The critique is organized around three lenses drawn from the course's critical framework:

1. **Formal Observation** — responding to the piece as a composition: color, motion, rhythm, pacing, emotional register. The tool doesn't matter; the image does.

2. **System Analysis** — examining what is authored by the student and what is generated. Where is the student's hand visible? Are the seams between human direction and machine generation legible?

3. **Consequences** — asking what source material and labor are embedded in the work. Does the piece acknowledge its origins? This lens is about awareness, not guilt.

Students arrive at the crit with a written statement, a prompt log excerpt showing pivotal moments in their AI collaboration, and specific questions they need the room to answer. They leave with concrete notes on what to improve before Bijou.

## The Pedagogy

The course is grounded in CalArts' lineage of experimental animation — the tradition of John and James Whitney, visual music, and computational cinema — while engaging directly with the conditions of working with AI tools in 2026.

Students are taught to use powerful tools while understanding what those tools are built on. The course addresses:

- **Creative agency** — students remain the authorial voice; the AI is a collaborator, not an author
- **Process legibility** — prompt logs make the creative process visible and accountable
- **Labor and technology** — readings and discussion on who benefits from new tools, drawing on labor history from the Luddites through contemporary debates about AI and creative work
- **Intellectual property** — situating vibecoding (trained on open-source code and technical writing) in contrast to image generation (trained on artists' work without permission)

The framing is neither uncritical embrace nor reflexive rejection of AI. Students learn to make sophisticated work *and* to articulate what is at stake in how they make it.

---

## What to Expect at the Screening

The pieces are short, vivid, and varied. Some are abstract and meditative; others are dynamic and reactive. A few incorporate audio or interactivity. All of them were made by students who, in most cases, had never written a line of code before January.

There will be no critique at the festival — just the work, presented as a body. If you're interested in the process behind any piece, the students' prompt logs are available and they are prepared to discuss their work in detail.
