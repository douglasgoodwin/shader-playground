## Vibecoding and the Crit
This class approaches synthetic work not as a separate genre, but as a set of image‑making conditions that distribute agency, legibility, labor, and consequence. The question is not whether a work is synthetic, but how synthesis is organized.

In this context, “brittle” systems are those whose seams, failures, and parameter limits remain visible and materially usable; glitches, artifacts, and breakdowns can become part of the work’s aesthetic vocabulary. “Smooth” systems are optimized for plausibility, completion, and frictionless finish; their interfaces are designed to hide the pipeline and suppress visible error. “Vibecoding” names a different relation to synthesis: the reintroduction of procedural legibility, iterative steering, and pipeline construction, even when highly automated or LLM‑mediated tools are involved. Rather than accepting the first plausible output, students learn to orchestrate models, prompts, scripts, interfaces, and edits into a discernible procedure.

Students do more than use tools, they create and build with them. They extract functions from one context and reapply them in another, turning effects into reusable procedures. This involves both style transfer and the transfer of functions. Example: behaviors such as audio reactivity can be isolated from one shader and redeployed elsewhere to drive a transformation (e.g. making a character appear to speak or causing a starfield to explode). In this way, students move from consuming output to constructing pipelines for novel works.

The crits are designed to give this work a more precise language. Each discussion moves through three lenses: what is in the work, how the system is organized, and what consequences are entangled with that system, including provenance, intellectual property, labor, and environmental impact. The aim is not to sort work into synthetic and non‑synthetic categories, but to understand more clearly how each pipeline organizes control, responsibility, and visibility.

## Crit Framework for Synthetic and Vibecoded Work
### Introduction

These crits treat synthetic work not as a separate genre, but as a set of image‑making conditions that distribute agency, legibility, labor, and consequence. The question is not whether a work is synthetic, but how synthesis is organized.

Some systems leave their seams visible and usable. Others are optimized for completion and frictionless finish. Some compress artistic decision‑making into narrow interfaces. Others reopen the pipeline and make procedure legible again. This framework allows handmade, composited, procedural, generative, and vibecoded work to be discussed together while preserving their differences.

Your opening statement should be brief and factual. Its purpose is to situate the work, not to explain it in advance or defend it before discussion begins.

### Shared Strata of Synthesis

**Handmade and captured**  
Drawing, painting, camera‑based, analog, optical, and composited work.

**Procedural and constructed**  
CGI, 3D modeling, motion graphics, scripting, and rule‑based or parametric systems.

**Generative and model‑driven**  
Early or visibly unstable systems, style‑transfer, GAN‑era work, and custom pipelines that retain recognizable seams or instability.

**Completion‑oriented generation**  
One‑shot systems optimized for finish, plausibility, and seamless illustration.

**Vibecoded systems**  
Works built through iterative scripting, orchestration, chaining, functional transfer, and interface‑level construction across multiple tools.

### Opening Statement
Keep this short and factual:
- Name the main strata of synthesis at work in this piece.  
- Identify the main tools, models, or software used.  
- Describe the main source material, data, or imagery involved.  
- Note any key functions or behaviors you have transferred from other contexts.  
- Mention any essential notes about authorship, intellectual property, or environmental impact.

### Crit Questions
1. **What is in the work?**  
   - What do you notice first?  
   - What holds up formally in the composition, timing, rhythm, color, texture, space, or affect?  
   - What feels specific, convincing, or surprising?  
   - What feels generic, overdetermined, unresolved, or thin?  
   - What remains meaningful if we temporarily set aside the question of how it was made?

2. **How is the system organized?**  
   - What appears to be authored, what appears to be automated, and what appears to be curated?  
   - Where does control seem to sit in this work?  
   - Are the seams of the system visible, hidden, or strategically mixed?  
   - If instability, artifacts, or glitches are present, are they functioning as real material or merely as style?  
   - If the work aims for smoothness or completion, what is gained and what is hidden?  
   - Does the artist simply use a tool, or transform it into part of a personal vocabulary?  
   - What functions, behaviors, or modules have been extracted, adapted, or transferred from another context?  
   - How much procedural legibility does the work retain?

3. **What consequences are entangled with the system?**  
   - What prior image worlds, styles, archives, or conventions seem embedded here?  
   - How clear is the provenance of the imagery, model, or dataset?  
   - What kinds of labor are present but not immediately visible?  
   - What questions of appropriation, licensing, scraping, or intellectual property seem to matter here?  
   - What environmental impact is part of this process, and does that impact seem necessary to the work?  
   - How do these conditions alter your reading of the work?

### Artist Reflection
- What was the most useful observation you heard?  
- What part of the conversation felt most accurate to the work?  
- What part of the conversation felt mistaken or projected?  
- What would you revise in the work or in the pipeline after this crit?

## From Tool Use to Tool Building: Toward a Critique of Synthetic Work

**Suggested section structure:**

1. **Why synthetic work needs a better crit language**  
   State the basic problem: synthetic practices are too often discussed as novelty, threat, or shortcut. Argue for a more precise language based on the organization of synthesis rather than medium panic.

2. **Synthesis as a continuum, not a rupture**  
   Place current practices in a longer history that includes analog compositing, optical effects, CGI, motion graphics, procedural systems, and earlier machine learning. Anchor the strata here and connect the framework to a broader CalArts history of constructed images and mediated processes.

3. **Brittle systems, smooth systems, and vibecoded systems**  
   Define the terms carefully and non‑polemically. Explain how brittle systems expose seams, how smooth systems suppress them, and how vibecoding reintroduces procedure, legibility, and iterative control.

4. **Functional transfer and procedural authorship**  
   This is where your classroom examples belong (e.g., the audio‑reactive shader). Emphasize that students are not merely using effects but extracting behaviors and redeploying them. Connect this to older notions of technique in animation, painting, and software practice: a once‑local trick becomes a repeatable move.

5. **Provenance, IP, labor, and environmental impact**  
   Explain why these are not external moral footnotes but internal conditions of the work’s production and meaning.

6. **Why crit should read image and system together**  
   Describe the classroom method: short opening statement, no confession booth, attention to the work before biography or defense, and a three‑part structure that moves from the formal to the systemic to the consequential.

7. **Pacing and tone in the room**  
   Address how to keep the conversation rigorous without making it punitive; how to avoid simple pro‑ or anti‑AI positions; and how to preserve room for disagreement across hand‑drawn, procedural, and generative practices.
