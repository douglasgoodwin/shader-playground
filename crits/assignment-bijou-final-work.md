# Assignment: Begin Your Bijou Piece

**FVEA-488-01: VibeCoding with AI**
**Assigned tonight. Due: before the pre-exhibition crit.**

---

## The Task

Start making the piece you will show at the Bijou Festival. You have 3 minutes of screen time. The audience will include people who have never seen a shader, never heard of vibecoding, and have no idea what GLSL is. The work has to land without explanation.

Tonight, begin. You don't need to finish. You need to *start* — with intention, not with wandering. If you have already begun, then great! I want you to answer the questions below in any case. 

---

## Step 1: Choose Your Starting Point (tonight)

Pick **one** of the following as the seed for your piece:

**A. A source.** A poem, a photograph, a sound, a memory, a piece of visual music you studied earlier this semester. Something outside the code that the code will translate. Write down what it is and why it matters to you in 2–3 sentences.

**B. A technique.** A shader behavior you've been working with that you want to push further — noise, particles, raymarching, symmetry, displacement, fluid. Write down which technique, what you've already done with it, and where you want to take it in 2–3 sentences.

**C. A question.** Something you're genuinely curious about and want to explore through the medium. "What does it look like when things try to stay together but can't?" "Can a shader feel slow?" "What happens if I give the AI contradictory instructions?" Write the question in one sentence.

Whichever you choose, **write it down tonight.** This is your anchor. You will return to it when you get lost, when the AI takes you somewhere unexpected, and when you need to decide what to keep and what to throw away.

---

## Step 2: Make Your First Version (this week)

Open shader-playground. Start a conversation with your preferred LLM. Begin working toward the piece.

Your goal for this first pass is not polish — it is **direction.** By the end of this session, you should be able to answer:

- What is this piece *about*? (Not what technique it uses — what is it about?)
- What is the strongest moment so far?
- What isn't working yet?

Record a 30–60 second MP4 of whatever you have at the end of your Thursday's session, even if it's rough.

---

## Step 3: Log Your Process (ongoing)

Keep your prompt log updated. As you work toward the final piece, flag exchanges that matter — the turns, the surprises, the decisions. You will need 2–3 of these for the crit.

Pay attention to:

- **False summits.** Moments where the AI gives you something plausible but wrong, and you have to back up. What did you do? Did you reset the conversation? Reframe the question? Try a different approach?
- **Your decisions.** Moments where the code could have gone two ways and you chose. Why?
- **Gaps.** Moments where what you asked for and what you got were different. Was the gap a problem, or did it open something up?

---

## Step 4: Think About the Crit (before you come in)

As you work, keep the crit in the back of your mind. You will be asked to present this piece to the room and hear honest feedback before Bijou. Start thinking now:

- **What will you say in your opening statement?** What is the most honest or surprising thing about how this piece came to be? Lead with that.
- **What questions do you actually need answered?** Not what sounds smart — what do you genuinely not know about your own work?
- **What would you change if you could?** Name it now, so you're not surprised when someone else names it in the crit.

You don't need to finalize these yet. But if you start thinking about them now, the work you make between tonight and the crit will be stronger for it. Making work with awareness of how it will be received is not selling out — it is composition.

## A Word About Ambition

You have access to a tool that lets you write code you couldn't write alone. That's powerful. It's also a trap.

The trap is breadth without depth: touching five techniques instead of committing to one. Generating ten variations instead of developing a single idea. The AI will happily produce an endless stream of plausible shaders. Your job is to choose — to decide what matters, to stay with it when it gets difficult, and to stop when it's done. You know this already, but you could talk about it. 

The best piece you can make for Bijou is probably not the most technically complex one. It is the one where your choices are visible, your intention is clear, and the viewer doesn't need to know what a shader is to feel something.

One more thing. Before the crit, you will need to show this piece to a room full of people and hear what they think. A CalArts MFA student named Fiona, after surviving a twelve-hour Asher crit, put it this way: "To get the most out of your crit, you have to have a mysterious blend of complete commitment to your decisions and total openness to reconsider everything." Come fully committed to the piece you made. Come fully open to hearing that it needs to change. Both at once. That's the work.

Start tonight.
